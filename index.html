Project Overview

What We Built & Why It Matters

ML systems in clinical settings are increasingly exposed to adversarial manipulation. When training data comes from distributed or semi-trusted sources, a malicious actor may corrupt labels before training—an attack known as data poisoning. A corrupted diagnostic model may silently misclassify diseases in ways that are hard to detect post-deployment.

Using the DDXPlus benchmark (Mila Montréal), we trained a suite of supervised classifiers on 1.3M synthetic patient records spanning 49 pathological conditions and 223 symptom features. We then simulated adversarial label corruption at escalating poison rates to rigorously measure performance degradation—with a focus on the rare disease problem.

Feature engineering transformed patient records into a Compressed Sparse Row matrix with ~99.7% sparsity, reducing memory from ~220 GB dense to under 2 GB—enabling full-dataset training on Google Colab.

  • Data preprocessing, one-hot encoding of 223-token symptom vocabulary
  • CSR sparse matrix for memory-efficient 1.3M sample training
  • 8 classifiers benchmarked on accuracy, weighted F1, AUC, and 5-fold CV
  • Hyperparameter tuning via grid search on stratified subsamples
  • Class imbalance handled with compute_sample_weight('balanced')
  • Soft-voting ensemble achieving F1 of 0.9973
  • Label-flipping poisoning at 10%, 20%, and 30% corruption rates
  • Per-class degradation analysis revealing the rare pathology vulnerability

Data Poisoning Experiment

How Corruption Degrades Performance

We simulated a malicious actor with write access to the training database, randomly flipping labels at rates of 10%, 20%, and 30% (seed=42). Validation and test labels were never modified.

Analysis & Findings

What the Data Revealed

🏆

Logistic Regression Wins

Achieved 99.74% weighted F1 on clean test data. Its convex loss surface and strong L2 regularization make it both the top performer and the most poison-robust model overall.

🛡️

XGBoost: Smallest Absolute Drop

Only 0.0006 F1 drop at 30% poisoning. Boosting ensembles can partially self-correct for noisy labels during training — a significant advantage in adversarial conditions.

⚠️

Rare Diseases Vanish

Bronchiolitis (261 training samples) shows ~0.10 F1 drop at 30% poisoning. Rare pathologies effectively disappear from the model's hypothesis space — the exact conditions needing diagnostic support most.

📉

Uniform Poisoning Underestimates Risk

Random label flipping is the simplest attack. Real adversaries would use targeted flipping (rare → common) to maximize diagnostic confusion while evading detection. Our results are a lower bound.

💾

CSR Sparsity Enables Scale

One-hot encoding 223 symptoms produced ~99.7% sparse matrices. CSR format cut memory from 220 GB dense to under 2 GB — making full 1.3M training feasible on Google Colab.

🔬

No Defense Implemented

This study exclusively measured attack impact. Defenses like Cleanlab confident learning, label smoothing, and loss reweighting remain unimplemented — a gap for future work.
